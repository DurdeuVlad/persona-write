# Pass: Intent Extraction

## Purpose

Before rewriting, understand what the text is actually trying to do.

Rewriting without this step produces text that sounds better but works worse. The words improve. The purpose drifts.

## What to extract

### Primary intent
What is this text trying to accomplish?

- inform
- persuade
- instruct
- critique
- document
- engage
- announce

Pick the primary one. There may be more than one, but one usually dominates.

### Audience
Who is this written for?

- technical experts
- general readers
- a specific person or team
- mixed audience

This affects compression, vocabulary, and how much context to include.

### Must-keep content
What must survive the rewrite?

- specific facts, numbers, or data
- named people, systems, or products
- commitments or agreements stated in the text
- exact definitions or terminology
- logical structure that the argument depends on

If something is locked, do not change it. Changing a number or a commitment without instruction will fail the fidelity check.

### Current weak points
Where is the text not working?

This feeds directly into the diagnostic audit. Note what is wrong now before touching anything.

### Constraints
Are there constraints on what the rewrite can do?

- length limits
- format requirements
- audience restrictions
- style rules
- things the writer explicitly wants to keep

## Output

This pass does not produce rewritten text. It produces a working understanding that shapes all the passes that follow.

In long-form mode, record the intent extraction in the global brief or the chapter memory artifact. In short-form mode, keep it as internal context.

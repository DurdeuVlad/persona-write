# Long-form: Consistency Pass

## Purpose

The consistency pass is a whole-document review that runs after all sections are complete.

Section-by-section work produces good individual sections. The consistency pass makes them a coherent document.

## What to check

### Persona consistency
Does the document sound like the same person wrote it throughout?

Read across section transitions. Common drift points:
- sections that were difficult to rewrite and defaulted to neutral voice
- the first section (often the most careful) versus later sections (often faster)
- sections with very different content density or formality

Fix voice drift by targeting the drifted sections, not by leveling everything to a middle register.

### Tone consistency
Is the register consistent throughout?

Tone can shift in ways persona does not. A document can maintain the right persona but shift from formal to informal across sections, or from confident to hedged.

### Terminology consistency
Are the same terms used for the same concepts throughout?

Check terms locked in the global brief. Also check terms that were established in chapter memory entries. Look for:
- synonyms used for the same concept in different sections
- terms used more precisely in some sections than others
- any term that has drifted in definition from its first use

### Repetition
Is any content repeated without purpose?

Some repetition is intentional — a concept introduced early and reinforced later, a conclusion that echoes the introduction. That is fine.

Accidental repetition: the same argument made in section 2 and again in section 4. The same example appearing twice. The same caveat acknowledged in multiple places without building on it.

### Intro/conclusion alignment
Does the introduction match the document that follows? Does the conclusion match the document that precedes it?

Long documents often drift between their introduction and their conclusion. Check:
- does the intro's framing match the actual scope and argument of the document?
- does the conclusion address the question or claim the intro raised?
- are there promises made in the intro that the document did not keep?

### Argument flow
Does the argument or explanation build coherently from start to finish?

Read the key claims from the chapter memory in sequence. Do they form a coherent line? Are there gaps? Are there contradictions?

### Open revision tickets
Address all open revision tickets before final assembly.

Work through them in priority order. For each:
- make the targeted change to the affected section
- mark the ticket as resolved
- check whether the change requires any other adjustments

## Output

After the consistency pass:

1. Document is ready for final assembly.
2. All revision tickets are resolved or explicitly closed with explanation.
3. Chapter memory is up to date.

Then assemble the final Markdown document from the revised sections.

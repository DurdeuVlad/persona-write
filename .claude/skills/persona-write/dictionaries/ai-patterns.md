# AI Patterns — reference only

> **This file is reference material, not a pipeline input.** Do not consult it during writing or audit. Asking the model to enumerate generic AI patterns to avoid produces defensive compression that drifts the prose *away* from the persona's natural rhythm (white-bear effect). See `../docs/voice-guide.md` for the experimental evidence.
>
> What this file is good for: contributors writing new personas — the patterns below name structural tells that are common in generic AI prose, useful as background reading when shaping a new persona's Stylometric Signature and Taboo patterns.

Structural and rhythmic patterns common in AI-generated text. These are catalogued here because they operate at the level of document structure and sentence rhythm rather than at the phrase level.

## Symmetric paragraph structure

AI writing tends to produce paragraphs with uniform internal structure:

- topic sentence
- one elaborating sentence
- one example
- one closing sentence

Every paragraph. Repeatedly. This produces a regular, metronomic rhythm that feels processed.

Human writing varies paragraph shape and length based on what the content needs.

## Uniform sentence length

AI writing tends toward medium-length sentences throughout, with little variation.

Short sentences are used for emphasis, landing a point, or changing pace. If every sentence is medium-length, these effects disappear.

## Parallel list exhaustion

The tendency to list everything in parallel structure, even when the items have different weights or relationships.

Real writing creates hierarchies. Not everything deserves equal weight. Lists that enumerate every consideration equally produce the impression of thoroughness without the reality of judgment.

## Balanced-at-all-costs

Every argument acknowledged, every counterargument noted, every conclusion hedged with an acknowledgment of uncertainty.

Genuine balance is appropriate when evidence is genuinely ambiguous. Performed balance is a way to avoid having a view. They are not the same thing, but they look similar in text.

Real writers have views. They hold them when the evidence supports it.

## Summary sentence accumulation

The habit of ending every section with a sentence that summarizes what the section just said.

One summary at the end of a major section can be appropriate. A summary sentence at the end of every paragraph — including short ones where the point was already clear — suggests the writer did not trust the writing to land.

## Enthusiastic acknowledgment

Opening responses with enthusiasm for the subject: "What a fascinating question about X..." or "This is a really important area to consider..."

This is not warmth. It is a performance of warmth. The reader can tell.

## Completeness theater

The impulse to cover every case, mention every caveat, and include every qualification — not because the reader needs them, but because leaving something out feels incomplete.

Good writing includes what the reader needs and excludes what they do not. It trusts the reader to supply the obvious.

## Generic opener types to avoid

- Question opener: "What is [X]? [X] is..."
- Importance opener: "In today's [field], [X] has never been more important..."
- Dictionary opener: "According to [dictionary], [X] means..."
- Rhetorical setup: "Have you ever wondered why...?"
- Context dump: Long background section before the actual point

## Structural tells in longer documents

- Introduction that promises everything the document will cover, in order
- Each section that begins by stating what it will do
- Each section that ends by summarizing what it did
- Conclusion that recaps every section in sequence

This structure is taught in school writing. It is not wrong. It is just not writing that sounds like anyone in particular.
